The Alexander Cipher — Eko Svenningsson

Chapter One

—

At 9:03 AM on Friday, November 7, Emma Vasquez logged into the Metropolitan Museum of Art's conservation database for the last time before the audit.

She had been there since 7:45. The department was empty at that hour — just the hum of climate control and the smell of linseed oil from the room next door. She liked the Museum before it became public. Before it remembered what it was for.

The piece in question was a Byzantine reliquary, twelfth century, silver and niello, catalogued under an acquisition number that predated the current system. Its provenance file had three gaps. Emma had been filling gaps for eleven years. This one, she'd been circling for six weeks.

She opened the provenance record and read it for what she estimated was the forty-third time.

The gaps were not accidents. That was the thing about gaps in provenance — they didn't occur naturally, the way organic matter decayed or silver tarnished. They were decisions. Someone had decided that this reliquary did not need a recorded history between 1923 and 1948. Someone had decided that the next documented owner — a Swiss foundation with a name that translated roughly to charitable trust for displaced cultural objects — did not need to be explained.

Emma was not supposed to be looking at this.

She looked anyway. That was also something she had been doing for eleven years.

—

At the same hour, in Crown Heights, Brooklyn, Miles Tatum was making coffee and trying not to check his phone.

He had a habit of checking his phone before he was fully awake. His therapist — former therapist — had called it a pre-emptive defense mechanism. Miles had pointed out that it was also just how information worked now. She had pointed out that not everything was an information problem. He had stopped going to therapy shortly after that.

The coffee machine finished. He poured. He checked his phone.

There was an email from a name he did not recognize, sent at 3:41 AM, subject line blank. The body contained one sentence:

You were found in the same file as someone who has been looking for you.

Miles set the mug down on the counter without drinking from it. He read the sentence again. Then he did what he had learned to do when something arrived that was designed to move him before he could think: he did nothing. He stood in his kitchen and let the information sit in the air between him and the screen without touching it.

He checked the sender domain. No website. No search results. The email address had been created forty-eight hours ago.

He put the phone face-down on the counter, picked up the coffee, and drank it standing up, looking at the window.

Outside, November moved through Brooklyn the way November did: without permission, without apology, leaving the light flat and the trees stripped to their structural logic. Miles had always found November clarifying. It was the month that removed ornament.

He turned the phone over and wrote back: Which file.

Then he went to work.

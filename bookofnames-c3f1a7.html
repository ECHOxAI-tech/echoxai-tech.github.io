Prologue

Every name is an act of preservation. To name is to insist on existence — to place a marker in the world that says: this person was here, and I witnessed it. The Book of Names began from a simple fear: that people disappear not because they die, but because no one holds them in language long enough for the shape of them to survive.

This collection operates on what I have come to call the inverse-Pessoa principle. Where Pessoa dispersed himself outward into many voices, I gather multiple voices inward — through unified narration, through the act of naming — to prevent disappearance. Each poem is a stay against forgetting. Each name placed in these pages becomes, in some small way, permanent.

Berlin is the ground on which this happens. Not because Berlin is special, though it is — but because it is the city in which I learned to witness. Its streets taught me that the irreversible exists everywhere: in the light that falls on Bergmannstraße at exactly 4 PM in October, in the canal at Urbanhafen that holds the sky without apology, in the faces of people who have decided to become exactly who they are and have nowhere left to hide.

✦

The collection moves through three artifacts: Water, which holds the moment before a name is given — baptism, transformation, the body before language; Mirror, which is the act of witnessing — to be seen is to be preserved, to see is to take on responsibility; and Pen, which is inscription itself — the irreversible act, the analog commitment, the mark that cannot be taken back.

These are not metaphors used decoratively. They are structural elements — the architecture that holds the collection together the way a building holds its inhabitants. Form and content cohere, or the poems fail. That is the discipline this work required.

To name the living is love.
To name the gone is the only form of resurrection
that does not require belief in anything
except language, and the willingness
to use it precisely.

What follows is a selection of poems from the collection. The full manuscript is publication-ready and available for submission inquiries.

From the Collection

I. Richie Goldfinger

Water — i

I write your name into the city

the way water writes itself into stone —

not decorating, not claiming,

just finding the path

that was always there.

Richie. The name lands differently

than it did when you were carrying it.

Now it belongs to the air

and to whoever reads this.

That is what I mean by preservation:

not that you are saved

but that you are placed —

in language, in light,

in the irreversible fact

of having been witnessed.

XI. Cath at Urbanhafen

Mirror — iii

The canal holds everything

without preference.

You leaned over the railing

and it held you too —

your face, inverted,

steadier than you felt.

I watched you watch yourself.

That is the whole poem.

The rest is just

what I remember of the light.

XXIV. The Golden Pen

Pen — xii

There is no undoing the mark.

That is the point of the pen —

not permanence as monument

but permanence as commitment:

I was here, I saw you,

I chose not to look away.

Every name in this book

is a line pressed into paper

hard enough to bruise.

Not for decoration.

For record.

The golden pen does not shine.

It works.
